Stay Cool

3 of 5

The body is always producing heat through metabolism, even when at rest, but during physical activity, working muscles produce heat at a much higher rate. When this increased rate of heat production occurs in a hot environment, the increase in body temperature can be difficult to control; therefore, it is important to find ways to cool the body down to help maintain core temperature and reduce the negative effects of heat stress.

  • 1
  • 2

Wear Appropriate Clothing

  • Wear light-colored, lightweight, loose-fitting clothing made from natural fibers like cotton and linen as well as a wide-brimmed hat and sunglasses.

Wear Sunscreen

  • Sunburn affects the body’s ability to cool down and can make you dehydrated.
  • Apply sunscreen of SPF 15 or higher 3 minutes before going out and reapply according to directions on the package. Look for sunscreens labeled “broad spectrum” or “UVA/UVB protection”.
  • Avoid energy drinks. Some contain much more caffeine than a standard serving of coffee, tea, or soft drinks; drinking several a day can affect the heart. Many also contain high amounts of sugar, which adds hundreds of extra calories to the diet.

Avoid Hot and Heavy Meals

  • Hot meals add heat to your body! Eat smaller meals more often and cold meals such as salad.

Limit/Modify Duration of Exposure

  • When possible schedule hot jobs for the cooler part of the day (early morning, late afternoon, or night) and/or schedule hot jobs on alternate rather than consecutive days.
  • Work as far away from radiant heat sources as possible.
  • Provide cool areas (e.g., air-conditioned or shaded) for rest and recovery. Air-conditioning increases air movement and reduces humidity, which aids in the evaporation of sweat, thereby cooling the body.
  • Permit work interruption when a worker feels heat discomfort.
