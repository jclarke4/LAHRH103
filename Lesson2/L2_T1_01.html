Syndromes, Symptoms, Predisposing Factors & First Aid

1 of 1
There are several different types of heat-related illness. They all occur when the body cannot cool itself adequately, but each is slightly different, and signs and symptoms vary based on the severity of the illness. With prompt treatment most people recover from heat-related illness; however, heat stroke can be fatal if not properly managed. Getting treatment in the early stages of a heat emergency can prevent the progression to heat stroke.

  • Heat Cramps
  • Heat Syncope
  • Rhabdomyolysis

Although heat rashes are not dangerous in themselves, they can impair areas of the skin and reduce sweating which reduces evaporative heat loss and affects thermoregulation. Wet and/or damaged skin can also absorb toxic chemicals more readily than dry, unbroken skin. There are three types of heat rash: miliaria rubra (‘prickly heat’), miliaria crystalline, and miliaria profunda (‘wildfire’).

Heat rash caused by obstructed sweat ducts


Miliaria rubra (‘prickly heat’)

The most common form of heat rash. It appears as red papules, usually in areas where clothing is restrictive, and causes a prickling sensation, particularly as sweating increases. It occurs in skin that is persistently wet from unevaporated sweat and is caused by mechanically obstructed sweat ducts. If untreated the papules may become infected and develop secondary staphylococcal infections. A rash with inflamed pus-filled papules is known as Miliaria pustulosa.

Miliaria crystalline

Appears when previously damaged skin begins sweating. It is common in sunburned areas of skin. The damage prevents the escape of sweat and causes the formation of water blisters. These water blisters quickly disappear once sweating stops and the problem goes away once the damaged skin is sloughed.

Miliaria profunda (wildfire)

Occurs when sweat ducts are blocked below the skin surface. This rash also occurs following a sunburn injury but has been reported to occur without clear evidence of previous skin injury. Discrete, pale elevations of the skin, resembling gooseflesh, are present.

Miliaria rubra (‘prickly heat’)

Red cluster of pimples or small blisters that usually appear on the neck, upper chest, groin, under the breasts, and in the elbow creases

Miliaria crystalline

Multiple tiny blister-like lesions that look like beads of perspiration that essentially cause no other symptoms

Miliaria profunda (‘wildfire’)
  • Extensive areas of skin which do not sweat on heat exposure, but present gooseflesh appearance, which subsides with cool environments.
  • Associated with incapacitation in heat
Miliaria rubra (‘prickly heat’)

Unrelieved exposure to humid heat with skin continuously wet with unevaporated sweat

Miliaria crystalline

Typically seen in newborns (up to 90%) in first week of life due to their underdeveloped sweat glands

Miliaria profunda (‘wildfire’)

Weeks or months of constant exposure to heat with previous history of extensive heat rash and sunburn

Miliaria rubra (‘prickly heat’)
  • When possible, a cooler, less humid environment is the best treatment. Keep the rash area dry.
  • Powder may be applied to increase comfort. Chlorhexidine lotion may provide relief. Itchiness generally responds well to antihistamines.
  • Wearing light, loose-fitting clothing will reduce the likelihood of developing heat rash.
Miliaria crystalline

No treatment necessary; will resolve on its own.

Miliaria profunda (‘wildfire’)

No effective treatment. Recovery of sweating occurs gradually on return to cooler climate.

Painful involuntary muscle contractions that usually affect people who sweat a lot during strenuous activity. The exact cause has not been determined. Heat cramps may also be a symptom of heat exhaustion.

  • Cramps often occur in the muscles principally used during work, including the legs, arms, and stomach.
  • Cramps may occur during physical activity or a rest period following physical activity.
  • Cramping is usually isolated to a specific muscle group.
  • Heavy sweating during hot work
  • Drinking large volumes of water without replacing the salt loss (Other electrolytes, such as magnesium, calcium, and potassium may also be involved)
  • Stop activity and move to a cool place.
  • Drink water and have a meal or snack, and/or carbohydrate-electrolyte replacement liquid (e.g., sports drinks) every 15-20 minutes to stay hydrated and to aid ingestion.
  • Avoid salt tablets. Salt tablets should never be used unless a person is instructed to do so by a doctor. Ingestion of too much salt may cause nausea and vomiting which can worsen the level of dehydration already present.
  • Get medical help if the person has heart problems, is on a low sodium diet, or if cramps do not subside within 1 hour.
  • Do not return to strenuous work for a few hours after the cramps subside.

  • Fainting (short duration)
  • Dizziness

A medical condition associated with heat stress and prolonged physical exertion resulting in the rapid breakdown, rupture, and death of muscle tissue. When muscle tissue dies, electrolytes and large proteins are released into the bloodstream. High levels of potassium, the main electrolyte released into the blood by muscle tissue death, can cause irregular and dangerous heart rhythms and seizures. Also, the large muscle proteins can damage the kidneys.

  • Muscle pain, cramping, swelling, and weakness
  • Decreased range of motion of the joints
  • Abnormally dark (tea or cola-colored) urine

Symptoms can vary between individuals with some experiencing nonspecific symptoms such as:

  • Fatigue
  • Exercise intolerance
  • Abdominal pain
  • Back pain
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Confusion
  • Asymptomatic

Because muscle cramps and dark urine after prolonged exercise may be the only symptom, rhabdomyolysis may be mistaken for another heat-related illness or dehydration.

  • Prolonged, intense physical exertion
  • Elevated body temperature (also associated with heat stroke)
  • Use of certain prescription medications (e.g., cholesterol-lowering statins and antidepressants) and over-the-counter medications (antihistamines, NSAIDs, omeprazole)
  • Use of certain dietary supplements like creatine and caffeine
  • Use of illicit drugs that can reduce blood flow to muscle tissue, such as cocaine and methamphetamine
  • Underlying medical conditions (e.g., lupus or sickle cell trait)
  • Direct injury to the muscle tissue (i.e., trauma, burns) or infections
  • Stop activity
  • Increase oral hydration (water preferred)
  • Seek immediate care at the nearest medical facility
  • Ask to be checked for rhabdomyolysis [i.e., blood sample analyzed for creatine kinase (CK) or creatine phosphokinase (CPK)]

Often considered a precursor to heat stroke. Heat exhaustion is the body’s response to an excessive loss of water and salt, usually through excessive sweating.

There are two types of heat exhaustion:

  • Water depletion heat exhaustion results from exposure to high temperature and insufficient fluid intake with the onset of symptoms occurring over a few hours.
  • Salt depletion heat exhaustion usually develops over several days; usually in people that replace fluid losses adequately but fail to replace salt losses.
  • Headache (water depletion)
  • Nausea or vomiting (salt depletion)
  • Dizziness (salt depletion)
  • Tiredness or weakness (water depletion)
  • Irritability
  • Thirst (water depletion)
  • Heavy sweating
  • Muscle cramps (salt depletion)
  • Fast, shallow breathing
  • Fast, weak pulse
  • Slightly elevated body temperature
  • Decreased urine output
  • Cold, pale, and clammy skin

Note: The symptoms of headache, nausea, dizziness, weakness, thirst, heavy sweating, and decreased urine output are common to both heat exhaustion and the early stage of heat stroke; however, people with heat exhaustion do not have altered mental status.

  • Advanced age
  • High blood pressure
  • Sustained exertion in the heat
  • Lack of acclimatization
  • Failure to replace water lost in sweat
  • Take the person to a clinic or emergency room for medical evaluation and treatment.
  • If medical care is unavailable, call 911.
  • Someone should stay with the person until help arrives.
  • Remove the person from the hot area and give liquids to drink.
  • Remove unnecessary clothing, including shoes and socks.
  • Cool the person with cold compresses or have the person wash head, face, and neck with cold water.
  • Encourage frequent sips of cool water.

The most serious type of heat-stress illness occurs when the body becomes unable to control its temperature. Body temperature rises rapidly, the sweating mechanism fails, and the body is unable to cool down. Body temperature can rise to 106℉ or higher within 10-15 minutes. Frequently, by the time a person is admitted to a hospital, the disorder has progressed to a multisystem emergency affecting virtually all tissues and organs. Heat stroke mortality rates range from 30-80% and it is universally fatal if left untreated; therefore, the presence of mental status changes in a hot and/or humid environment should be considered heat stroke until proven otherwise.

There are two types of heat stroke:

  • Classic (nonexertional) heat stroke is more commonly seen in infants, children, and older adults. It develops over a period of days, usually coinciding with a heat wave.
  • Exertional heat stroke usually develops rapidly in young, vigorously exercising individuals who have not acclimatized to a hot environment.

Note: there may be some overlap of symptoms between the two types of heat stroke.

Classic (Nonexertional) Heat Stroke
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Headache
  • Major disruption of the central nervous system including deteriorating mental status, unconsciousness, or convulsions
  • Lack of sweating (usually)
  • High body temperature
Exertional Heat Stroke
  • Nervous system changes ranging from severe headache to seizures to loss of consciousness
  • Profuse sweating (usually, not always)
  • Loss of coordination
  • High body temperature
  • Strong, fast pulse
  • Rhabdomyolysis
  • Sustained exertion in the heat
  • Obesity and lack of physical fitness
  • Recent alcohol intake
  • Dehydration
  • Individual susceptibility
  • Chronic cardiovascular disease
  • Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Call 911 for emergency medical care.
  • Stay with the person until emergency medical services arrive.
  • Move the person to a shaded, cool area, and remove outer clothing.
  • Cool the person quickly by wetting or applying ice to the head, neck, armpits, and groin areas.
  • Increase air movement to enhance evaporative cooling.
  • A person experiencing heat exhaustion or heat stroke should not be sent home or left unattended without a specific order from a responsible healthcare provider.
